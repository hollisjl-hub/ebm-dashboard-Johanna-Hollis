## Performance Metrics Analysis

### Problem-Related Metrics

#### Metric 1: Millennial/Gen Z Voluntary Turnover Rate
- **Current Value:** 23.4% annualized (based on past 12 months: Oct 2023-Sep 2024)
  - Millennials (born 1981-1996, ages 28-43): 21.7% turnover
  - Gen Z (born 1997-2012, ages 22-27): 27.8% turnover
  - Combined cohort: 156 voluntary departures out of 666 average headcount = 23.4%
- **Historical Trend (12 months):** **DECLINING (worsening)** - Shows concerning acceleration
  - Q4 2023: 19.2% annualized run rate
  - Q1 2024: 22.1% annualized run rate
  - Q2 2024: 24.7% annualized run rate  
  - Q3 2024: 25.9% annualized run rate (most recent quarter shows problem intensifying)
- **Benchmark Comparison:** 
  - vs. Industry standard (SHRM 2024): 18.1% for professional services sector = **We are 29% ABOVE industry average**
  - vs. Internal target: 15% = **We are 56% ABOVE internal goal**
  - vs. Organizational overall turnover: 14.2% = **Millennial/Gen Z rate is 65% higher than company average**
- **Problem Connection:** This is the core metric demonstrating the retention crisis exists. Rate has risen 35% over past year (from 19.2% to 25.9% quarterly run rate). Problem is concentrated in younger employees - Gen Z turnover nearly double company average. Financial impact: 156 departures × $25,000 avg replacement cost = $3.9M annual cost to organization.
- **Data Source:** Workday HRIS - Filtered for employees born 1981-2012, calculated voluntary terminations (excluding retirements, involuntary separations, internal transfers) divided by average headcount over period. Verified against HR monthly dashboard reports.
- **Data Quality:** **High reliability.** Termination dates and birth dates are verified fields in Workday. Voluntary vs. involuntary termination reason codes are assigned by HR during off-boarding and regularly audited. Only limitation: doesn't capture employees who mentally "quit" but haven't yet left (hidden turnover).

#### Metric 2: Average Tenure at Departure (Millennials/Gen Z)
- **Current Value:** 18.3 months median tenure for millennial/Gen Z voluntary departures
  - Distribution: 
    - <12 months: 34% of departures (early regret/bad fit)
    - 12-24 months: 41% of departures (post-honeymoon/development frustration window)
    - 24-36 months: 18% of departures
    - 36+ months: 7% of departures
  - Mean tenure: 21.6 months (skewed by a few longer-tenured departures)
- **Historical Trend (12 months):** **DECLINING (worsening)** - Employees leaving earlier in their tenure
  - 2022: 24.1 months median
  - 2023: 20.8 months median
  - 2024: 18.3 months median (represents 24% decrease in just 2 years)
- **Benchmark Comparison:**
  - vs. Industry data (LinkedIn Workforce Report): 2.8 years median for millennials in professional services = 33.6 months vs. our 18.3 months = **We lose millennials 45% faster than industry**
  - vs. Organizational median: 4.2 years overall = 50.4 months = **Millennial/Gen Z employees stay 64% less time than average**
  - vs. "Productive tenure" threshold: ROI breakeven estimated at 18-24 months = **Many employees leave right when they become fully productive**
- **Problem Connection:** Short tenure means organization barely recoups training/onboarding investment before employee leaves. The 12-24 month departure spike suggests inadequate career development and growth opportunities during critical "second year" phase. Concentration of early departures (<12 months) suggests possible onboarding failures or misaligned expectations.
- **Data Source:** Workday HRIS - Calculated months between hire date and termination date for millennial/Gen Z voluntary departures. Cross-referenced with exit interview timing to confirm these represent fully exited employees (not extended notice periods).
- **Data Quality:** **High reliability.** Hire dates and termination dates are verified HR system fields. Clean data with no missing values. Limitation: Doesn't account for previous tenure if employee left and returned (rare but possible).

#### Metric 3: Estimated Annual Cost of Millennial/Gen Z Turnover
- **Current Value:** $3.94 million annual cost (based on 12-month period Oct 2023-Sep 2024)
  - Calculation: 156 departures × $25,270 average replacement cost
  - Cost breakdown per departure:
    - Recruiting costs: $4,200 (job ads, agency fees, recruiting team time)
    - Onboarding/training: $6,800 (new hire orientation, role training, systems access, training team time)
    - Lost productivity: $12,500 (assuming 6 months to full productivity at 75% of annual salary)
    - Knowledge loss: $1,770 (time existing employees spend answering questions, recreating institutional knowledge)
  - Range: Conservative estimate $3.1M (using lower multiplier) to aggressive estimate $6.2M (using 1.5× salary multiplier)
- **Historical Trend (12 months):** **DECLINING (worsening - costs increasing)**
  - 2022: $2.1M (estimated 98 departures × $21,400 avg cost)
  - 2023: $3.2M (estimated 131 departures × $24,400 avg cost)
  - 2024: $3.9M (actual 156 departures × $25,270 avg cost)
  - **Represents 86% cost increase in just 2 years** due to both volume increase and rising replacement costs (competitive market driving higher salaries)
- **Benchmark Comparison:**
  - vs. % of payroll: $3.94M / $42M total millennial/Gen Z payroll = **9.4% of payroll spent replacing these employees** (industry acceptable range: 3-5%)
  - vs. % of revenue impact: If organization is $200M revenue, this represents **2.0% of revenue** lost to turnover costs
  - vs. Opportunity cost: $3.94M could fund 157 employees at $25K each in training/development investments - equivalent to comprehensive manager training program or significant compensation equity adjustments
- **Problem Connection:** Financial magnitude demonstrates this isn't just "normal" turnover - it represents material business impact. Cost acceleration (86% increase in 2 years) shows problem is worsening faster than inflation. Payroll percentage double the acceptable range proves this requires executive attention and investment to solve.
- **Data Source:** Finance reports (recruiting spend from applicant tracking system, training costs from LMS and Finance records), HR estimates for lost productivity (based on manager survey feedback), turnover counts from Workday. Replacement cost methodology validated with Finance Director using standard cost accounting principles.
- **Data Quality:** **Medium reliability.** Hard costs (recruiting, training) are actual data - high quality. Lost productivity is estimated using industry standards - medium quality (actual impact could be higher or lower depending on role complexity). Doesn't capture all indirect costs (e.g., impact on team morale, customer satisfaction, project delays). Conservative calculation - actual total impact likely higher.

#### Metric 4: Exit Interview Themes (Millennial/Gen Z Departures)
- **Current Value:** Analysis of 106 exit interviews from millennial/Gen Z voluntary departures (68% completion rate)
  
  **Top Departure Reasons (respondents could select multiple):**
  1. **Limited career advancement opportunities:** 73% (77/106) - "No clear path forward"
  2. **Manager/leadership concerns:** 61% (65/106) - "Relationship with direct manager" cited as factor
  3. **Better opportunity elsewhere:** 58% (61/106) - Competing offer with more responsibility/growth
  4. **Compensation:** 52% (55/106) - Though often secondary to development concerns
  5. **Work-life balance/flexibility:** 41% (43/106) - Desire for remote work, flexible hours
  6. **Company culture concerns:** 28% (30/106) - "Doesn't feel like right fit"
  
  **Qualitative Themes from Open-Ended Comments (thematic coding):**
  - **Development focus:** "Felt stuck," "no mentorship," "same work for 18 months with no growth," "asked for new challenges but told to 'wait your turn,'" "watched external hire get promoted into role I wanted"
  - **Manager quality:** "Micromanaged," "no regular 1-on-1s," "manager doesn't understand my career goals," "toxic team environment," "favoritism," "don't feel valued by manager"
  - **Speed/pace:** "Too slow to make decisions," "bureaucracy frustrating," "can't innovate," "want to make bigger impact faster than possible here"
  - **Flexibility:** "Required in office 4 days when role could be remote," "can't adjust hours for personal needs," "other companies offering full remote"
  
- **Historical Trend (12 months):** **STABLE themes** - Career development and manager quality have been top 2 reasons consistently over past 3 years. Flexibility concerns have increased from 22% in 2022 to 41% in 2024 (87% increase - growing factor).
- **Benchmark Comparison:**
  - vs. Gallup research: Aligns with external evidence - career development (87% of millennials prioritize), manager quality (manager accounts for 70% of variance in engagement), flexibility (71% want work-life balance)
  - vs. Internal comparison: For non-millennial/Gen Z exits, compensation is #1 reason (68%) and career development is lower (41%) - **Different generational priorities confirmed**
- **Problem Connection:** Validates that external research applies to our organization. Career development and manager quality are organizational control areas (unlike pure compensation which is market-driven). Concentration of these themes gives clear direction for solution design. Qualitative comments reveal specific pain points: lack of regular development conversations, slow pace for ambitious employees, inconsistent manager practices.
- **Data Source:** Exit Interview Database - HR conducts structured exit interviews with all voluntary departures; interviews coded by HR analyst using standard taxonomy. Supplemented with open-text analysis using thematic coding. 106 interviews analyzed out of 156 total departures (68% completion rate).
- **Data Quality:** **Medium reliability.** Completion rate is decent but 32% of departures didn't complete interviews - likely includes most negative departures who don't want to provide feedback (survivorship bias). Social desirability bias: departing employees may soften criticism to preserve references. Strength: consistent interview protocol over time allows trend analysis. Limitation: reasons are self-reported and may not capture full picture (e.g., employee says "compensation" but real issue might be underappreciation).

  - High performers represent 23% of overall millennial/Gen Z population (153 high performers / 666 employees)
  - **High performers are 1.28x more likely to leave** than average performers (disproportionate loss of talent)

- **Historical Trend (12 months):** **DECLINING (worsening)** - Losing higher % of high performers over time
  - 2022: 18% of departures were high performers
  - 2023: 24% of departures were high performers
  - 2024: 29.5% of departures were high performers
  - **64% increase in high-performer loss rate in 2 years**

- **Benchmark Comparison:**
  - vs. Expected: If turnover were random, 23% of departures should be high performers (proportional to population) = **We're losing high performers at 28% above expected rate**
  - vs. Succession planning impact: 46 high-performer departures = **30% of succession pipeline lost** (organization identified 152 high-potentials; 46 left = 30.3%)
  - vs. Cost impact: High performers estimated 2-3x replacement cost of average performers = $35-40K per high-performer departure vs. $20-25K average performer

- **Problem Connection:** Not only is turnover high, but we're losing our best talent disproportionately. This suggests problem isn't just "bad fit" employees leaving - it's employees we want to retain. Exit interview data for high performers shows even stronger emphasis on career development (88% cited vs. 73% overall) - high performers feel growth-constrained. - **Data Source:** Workday performance ratings (most recent annual review cycle) cross-referenced with turnover data. Performance ratings: Outstanding (5%), Exceeds Expectations (18%), Meets Expectations (68%), Needs Improvement (8%), Unsatisfactory (1%). "High performer" defined as Exceeds or Outstanding.

- **Data Quality:** **Medium reliability.** Performance ratings are somewhat subjective and subject to rating inflation. Managers may be generous with ratings, meaning "Exceeds" may be closer to "truly Meets." Limitation: Rating is snapshot in time (annual review) and employee performance may have changed before departure. Strength: Ratings are calibrated across departments in annual process, providing some consistency.

### Process Analysis

#### Current State Process Documentation
**Process Name:** Career Development Planning & Manager Conversations

- **Process Description:** How career development currently happens (or doesn't):
  1. Annual performance review includes "Career Development Goals" section (1 page in review form)
  2. Manager supposed to discuss development in annual review meeting (60-90 min conversation)
  3. Employee can document 2-3 development goals in system
  4. Manager supposed to follow up quarterly (no system reminder or accountability)
  5. No formal career pathing tools or frameworks provided to managers
  6. No training for managers on how to have development conversations
  7. No tracking of whether development discussions actually occur beyond annual review
  8. No connection between development goals and actual opportunities/assignments
  9. Promotion decisions happen separately from development planning (ad hoc)
  10. Employee can request mentor through informal process (no matching system)

- **Process Performance:** Current efficiency, quality, cost metrics
  - Completion rate: 89% of employees have documented development goals in system (compliance metric)
  - **BUT:** Quality assessment: Goals are often generic ("improve communication skills," "learn new technology") rather than specific/actionable
  - Manager survey (2023): Only 42% of managers say they "regularly" discuss development beyond annual review
  - Employee survey (2024): Only 48% of millennials/Gen Z agree "my manager helps me develop my skills"
  - Follow-through rate: Unknown - no system tracking whether goals lead to actual development activities
  - Cost: Minimal - process is documentation-focused, not action-focused (explains why completion is high but satisfaction is low)

- **Problem Manifestation:** How the problem shows up in this process
  - Process is "check the box" compliance exercise rather than meaningful development conversation
  - Managers lack skills/tools to have effective career discussions (training gap)
  - No accountability for manager follow-through after annual review
  - Employees don't see connection between documented goals and actual opportunities
  - Development is reactive (employee asks) rather than proactive (manager initiates)
  - High performers feel "stuck" because process doesn't create path forward
  - Exit interview themes reflect this: "asked for development but nothing happened," "goals written but never discussed again"

- **Improvement Potential:** What organizational data suggests about improvement opportunities
  - **Low-hanging fruit:** Add quarterly system reminders to managers about development check-ins
  - **Medium effort:** Create career framework showing paths forward by role/function
  - **Higher effort:** Train managers on effective development conversations (biggest impact potential)
  - **Infrastructure:** Add tracking to measure whether development conversations happen beyond annual review
  - **Culture shift:** Link manager performance ratings to team development/retention metrics (accountability)

#### Historical Process Performance
- **Baseline Performance:** What performance looked like before problem emerged (2020-2021)
  - Development goal completion: 84% (similar to today - compliance was always decent)
  - Manager effectiveness on development dimension: 3.6/5.0 (slightly lower than today's 3.8 - modest improvement)
  - Employee satisfaction with development: Est. 62% favorable (vs. 48% today for millennials/Gen Z - significant decline)
  - Turnover rate: 12.1% overall, 15.3% for millennials/Gen Z (lower than today)

- **Performance Decline Pattern:** How and when performance deteriorated
  - 2020-2021: Development satisfaction relatively strong post-pandemic (employees grateful for employment security)
  - 2022: Satisfaction began declining as job market heated up and employees gained leverage
  - 2023: Turnover accelerated (19.2%) as competitors offered aggressive opportunities
  - 2024: Problem reached crisis level (23.4%) - development gap now costing organization significantly

- **Contributing Factors:** What internal data suggests about causes
  - Market shift: Competitive labor market raised employee expectations for development
  - Expectation gap: Manager capability didn't keep pace with rising employee expectations
  - Process stagnation: Career development process hasn't evolved in 5+ years while employee needs changed
  - Priority shift: Organizational focus on growth/revenue overshadowed talent development investment
  - Manager bandwidth: Increased business demands left less time for development conversations

- **Previous Improvement Attempts:** What's been tried and what were the results
  - 2022: Added "development goals" mandatory field to performance review form → **Result:** Increased documentation compliance but didn't change behavior or satisfaction
  - 2023: Launched optional online career resources library → **Result:** Low utilization (18% accessed) - too passive/self-service
  - 2024: CEO messaging emphasizing talent development importance → **Result:** Raised awareness but didn't provide managers with tools/skills to change practice
  - **Lesson:** Passive/awareness-focused interventions insufficient - need structured, accountable process changes with manager skill-building

### Synthesis of Organizational Evidence

#### Strongest Evidence for Problem Existence
The internal data provides overwhelming, convergent evidence that millennial/Gen Z retention is a significant, worsening problem:

1. **Magnitude:** 23.4% turnover rate is 29% above industry average and 65% higher than company overall (156 departures annually)

2. **Financial Impact:** $3.94M annual cost = 2% of revenue and 9.4% of cohort payroll (double acceptable range)

3. **Trend:** Problem accelerating - 35% increase in turnover rate over past year; 86% increase in costs over 2 years

4. **Talent Loss:** Losing high performers at 1.28x rate vs. average performers; 30% of succession pipeline lost

5. **Root Cause Confirmation:** Exit interview themes (73% cite career development, 61% cite manager) match exactly with engagement survey low scores (career development dimension 14 points below average, manager dimension 11 points below)

6. **Predictability:** 2022 report predicted current state accurately - problem was foreseen but not addressed, now manifesting as predicted

7. **Cohort-Specific:** Problem concentrated in millennials/Gen Z (23.4%) vs. overall company (14.2%) - not a universal turnover issue

**Evidence Quality:** High convergence across multiple independent data sources (HRIS, exit interviews, engagement surveys, financial data) tells consistent story. Not anecdotal - quantitative, trend-based, benchmarked.

#### Strongest Evidence for Solution Feasibility
Internal data suggests organization has capacity to implement evidence-based retention solution:

1. **Resource Availability:** $420K budgeted + $150K discretionary possible = $570K available for solution investment (covers estimated $300-400K solution cost)

2. **Leadership Support:** CEO mentions in meetings, budget increase approved, HR has elevated access - stated priority backed by resource allocation

3. **Foundation Capability:** Manager effectiveness baseline at 3.8/5.0 (not starting from zero), 78% LMS engagement, 71% overall engagement = foundation exists to build upon
